# 02 — Character Design

## Complete Character Creation Checklist

### 1. Basics
Name, age, sex, occupation, marital status, appearance. The foundation — not exciting, but necessary.

### 2. A Limp and an Eyepatch
A simple physical trait or mannerism that differentiates them. Harry Potter characters are a masterclass: Hagrid's size, Dumbledore's half-moon spectacles, Snape's greasy hair, Luna's dreaminess.

### 3. Emotion Evoked
What should the audience feel when they see this character? Empathy for the protagonist, hatred for the villain, warmth for a mentor, humor from comic relief. Design other elements to serve this emotional target.

### 4. Traits
Positive and negative surface characteristics — skills, fears, personality. These are what people notice first.

### 5. Values / Ideology
What does the character believe in? Family? Freedom? Honor? Cynicism? This shapes their decisions and worldview. Focus on values that relate to the story's theme.

### 6. Flaw
Not a superficial weakness (bad at soccer) but a **psychological error** — a mindset that hinders them. This flaw is punished by obstacles until it's overcome (positive arc) or leads to downfall (tragic arc). The flaw should be thematically connected.

### 7. Past Wound
An event that shaped their worldview. Usually the cause of the flaw. Provides backstory reveal opportunities and justifies why the character is the way they are. Doesn't have to be traumatic — could be inspirational — but the "wound" framing is useful.

### 8. Secret
Something hidden from other characters. For the MC, the audience usually knows (maximizes suspense of discovery). For supporting characters, save it for a reveal (maximizes surprise). Examples: Walter White's meth business, Snape's love for Lily, Vader's fatherhood.

### 9. Goals + Stakes
Every character needs a goal, and every goal needs stakes (reward for success, consequence for failure). Conflicting goals between characters generate plot.

### 10. Want vs Need
- **Want**: Conscious motivation. What the character thinks they need. The obvious motivator.
- **Need**: Hidden motivation. What they truly yearn for deep down — love, survival, acceptance, control, respect.

Initially, Want and Need push toward the same Goal. As the story progresses, they come into conflict. The character's choices in those moments reveal who they truly are.

*Example*: Walter White's Want = provide for his family. His Need = power, control, respect. When forced to choose, he always chooses the meth empire over his family — revealing his true self.

### 11. Arc
Which of the 5 arc types? (See Arc Types section below.)

## Building Complex Characters: Contradictions

Most characters have coherent traits/values/motivations. Complex characters have **cognitive dissonance** — their elements contradict each other, just like real people.

**Tony Soprano** — the gold standard:
- Mob boss who sees a therapist
- Appears emotionless, but cries in therapy
- Seems emotional in sessions, but acts as a sociopathic killer
- Genuinely wants to be better for his wife, but can't stop cheating

Contradictions make characters layered, unpredictable, and real. Look for opportunities to make a character's job contradict their values, personality contradict their goals, or motivations contradict each other.

## Revealing Character (8 Methods)

You can't just state traits — you must show them:

1. **Name & description** — Connotations matter. "Wilford Humphrey III" suggests aristocracy without saying it.
2. **Contrast with other characters** — Want to show bravery? Put the character next to someone who runs away. Create foil characters with opposite traits.
3. **Contrast with environment** — Fish out of water scenarios naturally reveal traits through friction.
4. **How others talk about them** — Build anticipation before introduction (Hannibal Lecter is warned about before we meet him).
5. **How others react to them** — Does the room go quiet? Do people light up? Fear? Reverence?
6. **Dialogue** — Speech patterns reveal mood, background, education, ideology, power.
7. **Actions, reactions, decisions** — The ultimate character reveal. When conflict forces a choice, we see who they really are.
8. **Mannerisms** — Physical behavior reflects psychology. A shy person holds themselves differently than a confident one.

## Web of Conflict

Before outlining your plot, list every possible pairing of main/supporting characters. Find conflict in every pairing — conflicting goals, traits, values, wants, flaws. This ensures no two characters are alike and lays groundwork for dialogue, scenes, and drama across the entire story.

## Building the Empathetic Hero

### 8 Likeable Characteristics (from Jamie Nash)
1. **Underdog** — Doesn't fit in, at a disadvantage, lacks resources
2. **Cares about someone/something** — Supports a cause, loves deeply, values something
3. **Tries hard** — Has a dream, works toward it, tries again after failure
4. **Fun** — Funny, charming, charismatic, attractive
5. **Struggling** — Pain, depression, anxiety, boredom — relatable struggles
6. **Admirable** — Traits we wish we had: altruistic, optimistic, loyal, honest
7. **Just like us** — Normal problems, relatable secrets, everyday situations
8. **Skilled** — Great at something: singing, fighting, deduction, driving

### Three Empathy Amplifiers (from Karl Iglesias)

**Victimization** — Make the audience feel sorry for them through undeserved suffering: embarrassment, injustice, discrimination, false accusation, abuse, loss, betrayal, abandonment, rejection, loneliness, not being believed. The suffering must be undeserved.

**Virtue** — Show them doing good: helping others, being polite, forgiving, self-sacrificing, fighting for a cause, persevering, getting along with children and animals, loving others.

**Desirable Qualities** — Even un-empathetic characters become interesting with: power, charisma, cool profession, courage, skills, humor, intelligence, rebelliousness, eccentricity.

**Balance**: Give every protagonist some victimhood, some virtue, and some desirable qualities. Avoid making them purely a victim (no agency) or purely virtuous (no complexity). And never let them whine — audiences like characters who push through without complaint.

## Building the Antagonist

The antagonist's defining requirement: **a goal that conflicts with the MC's goal**. Beyond that, they exist on a moral spectrum:

- **Morally good antagonist**: FBI agent chasing a sympathetic scammer (*Catch Me If You Can*)
- **Complex antagonist**: Has an admirable goal but terrible methods (Thanos)
- **Purely evil antagonist**: No redeeming qualities (Voldemort)

Fit the villain to the story's tone. Moral ambiguity stories → complex villains. Good vs evil stories → pure villains.

### Making the Antagonist More Hateable
- **Kill the Cat** — Show them doing something cold-hearted to something innocent or defenseless
- **Kill the Audience** — Attack the audience vicariously by humiliating, hurting, or killing an empathetic character

### Antagonist Complexity
Same as any character: contradictions and inner conflict, especially between Want and Need. Villains can also have arcs (corruption origin story, or redemption).

## The 5 Character Arc Types

Every arc involves a battle between a **Truth** and a **Lie** across the story's theme. The writer assigns which side of the thematic argument is the "truth" and which is the "lie." The character's **Want** drives their initial belief; their **Need** determines their final belief.

### 1. POSITIVE CHANGE ARC
Starts believing the Lie → Overcomes it → New Truth is liberating
- *Examples*: Han Solo, Cooper (Interstellar), Jaime Lannister

### 2. FLAT ARC
Starts believing the Truth → Maintains it → Uses Truth to overcome the world's Lie
- *Examples*: Batman (The Dark Knight), Buddy (Elf), Forrest Gump
- Note: The character doesn't change — the world around them does

### 3. DISILLUSIONMENT ARC
Starts believing the Lie → Overcomes it → New Truth is tragic
- *Examples*: Rick Grimes, Ned Stark

### 4. FALL ARC
Starts believing the Lie → Clings to it → Believes a worse Lie
- *Examples*: Anakin Skywalker, Jay Gatsby

### 5. CORRUPTION ARC
Starts seeing the Truth → Rejects it → Embraces the Lie
- *Examples*: Michael Corleone, Walter White

## Static Characters as Thematic Voices

Supporting characters who don't change still serve a vital function: they represent different perspectives on the theme. One might believe the Lie successfully. Another might believe the Truth. Another takes an unexpected third position. Another is indifferent.

These static viewpoints come into conflict with each other and push dynamic characters toward change. Think of them as the angel and devil on every dynamic character's shoulder.
