# 03 — Theme & Story Structure

## Crafting Your Theme

A theme is a philosophical argument underlying the plot, characters, and arcs. Three formats:

### Format 1: Yes/No Philosophical Question
Can people change? Does the greater good trump individual rights? Is freedom more important than safety? Is lying ever moral? Does morality exist? Can utopia ever exist? Is war ever justifiable? Does objective truth exist? Should AI have rights? Do people have free will?

### Format 2: Thematic Statement (True or False?)
Ignorance is bliss. Technology is evil. Love never lasts. Family is a blessing. Money can't buy happiness. Power is an illusion. Being honorable is a weakness. People can be trusted. There is beauty in everything. Mankind is a disease.

### Format 3: X vs Y
Good vs evil. Order vs chaos. Cynicism vs idealism. Faith vs skepticism. Hope vs despair. Logic vs love. Passion vs money. Morality vs nihilism. Love vs duty. Nature vs industrialization. Courage vs fear.

### How Theme Works

Assign one side as **Truth**, one as **Lie** (your creative choice). The story explores this through specifics: plot dilemmas are thematically charged, wrong choices (Lie) produce setbacks, the right choice (Truth) comes at the climax. Static characters represent various positions on the theme. The ending delivers the story's verdict.

**Avoiding preaching**: Show through action and consequence, never speeches. Let the audience discover the argument. Extra subtlety for politically sensitive themes.

## Three-Act Structure Basics

**Act 1 (~25%)**: Set up status quo. Disrupt with inciting incident (~12.5% mark). MC forced to act.

**Act 2 (~50%)**: MC pursues goal, encounters constant setbacks, struggles toward the Truth. Major turning point at midpoint.

**Act 3 (~25%)**: Final struggle — goal achieved or not, character evolved or fallen.

### Available Structure Methods

All variations on three acts — pick what resonates, or combine:

- **Save the Cat! Beat Sheet** (15 beats) — Most prescriptive
- **8-Reel/Sequence Structure** (8 sequences) — Breaks Act 2 into chunks
- **Hero's Journey** (17 stages) — Best for mythological/adventure
- **Dan Harmon's Story Circle** (8 steps) — Simplified, circular
- **Freytag's Pyramid** (5 steps) — Classical, minimal
- **7-Point Structure** (7 beats) — Good middle ground

### The Emotional Rollercoaster

Alternate success and failure for the MC. If a scene starts well, it should end badly (and vice versa). All plotlines ride the same rollercoaster — at an "all is lost" moment, everything goes wrong together.

## Scene-Sequel Structure (Dwight Swain)

More important than overall structure. Scenes alternate between two types in a loop:

### SCENE (external conflict → plot)
- **GOAL**: What does the focal character want to accomplish?
- **CONFLICT**: What obstructs the goal? (Bulk of the scene. No conflict = cut it.)
- **DISASTER**: Character doesn't get what they want cleanly — failure, or success with a costly catch.

### SEQUEL (internal conflict → character)
- **REACTION**: Character responds to the disaster (reveals who they are)
- **DILEMMA**: They recognize options — ideally an agonizing choice pitting Want vs Need
- **DECISION**: They choose, revealing character and setting up the next Scene's goal

### Pacing Through Scene-Sequel
- Scenes = tension, plot, external stakes
- Sequels = breathing room, character depth, internal stakes
- A-story told in Scenes; B-story (love interest, mentor) often provides Sequels
- Alternation prevents relentless tension or boring reflection

### Why This Matters
Ensures every scene has conflict, something changes, decisions drive plot (not coincidence), tension ratchets up.
