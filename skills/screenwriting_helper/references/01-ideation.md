# 01 — Ideation & Concept Development

## Four Approaches to Generating Story Ideas

Each approach starts from a different seed. None is better — pick the one that matches what you already have.

### 1. Theme-First
Start with a philosophical claim about the world. Build a plot and characters that test that claim.

**Process**: Pick a theme → Create characters who embody opposing sides → Design a plot that forces the argument to play out → The resolution is your "answer" to the theme.

*Example*: "Order is better than chaos" → Batman embodies order, Joker embodies chaos → The Dark Knight's plot tests whether order or chaos prevails.

### 2. Character-First
Start with a flawed person who must change. Build a world that forces that change.

**Process**: Create a character with a specific flaw → Ask "what situation would force them to confront this flaw?" → That situation becomes your plot → The change becomes your theme.

*Example*: Walter White — an undervalued chemistry teacher with no control over his life → Terminal cancer diagnosis forces him to act → Cooking meth gives him power → Theme emerges: cautionary tale about ego and greed.

### 3. World/Concept-First
Start with "what if...?" or an intriguing setting.

**Process**: Define the world's unique properties → Populate with characters shaped by that world → Create an inciting incident that disrupts the status quo → Let the world naturally suggest themes.

*Example*: "What if children's toys were alive?" → Toys fear being replaced → New toy arrives → Themes of jealousy, loyalty, identity emerge naturally.

### 4. Plot-First
Start with an inciting incident or a sequence you want to see.

**Process**: Define the inciting incident → Map out key scenes, sequences, set-pieces → Create characters most suited for that journey → Discover what themes underlie the journey.

## High Concept Formulas

A high concept story has a unique premise that's easy to pitch and naturally generates conflict. Three templates:

### Formula 1: Spice Up the Generic
> There's a _____ (setting/character/situation) but _____ (unexpected twist).

- Theme park but with real dinosaurs → *Jurassic Park*
- School but for wizards → *Harry Potter*

### Formula 2: Twist an Existing Idea
> It's _____ (existing story) but _____ (new element).

- *Castaway* but on Mars → *The Martian*
- Monster movie but in space → *Alien*
- Mafia show but the boss sees a therapist → *The Sopranos*

### Formula 3: Twist Reality
> What if _____ (everyday thing) was _____ (different)?

- What if toys were alive? → *Toy Story*
- What if reality was a simulation? → *The Matrix*

**Why high concept matters**: It makes scenes easier to write (the concept itself generates unique conflict), it's easier to pitch, and it provides a crutch if other craft skills are still developing.

## Idea Expansion Techniques

- Combine two contradictory concepts
- Start from genre, then subvert genre conventions
- Pull from personal life experience
- Create a character with an obsessive goal
- Create a setting the audience/MC can discover together
- Put a twist on an existing story
- Subvert a trope or cliché
- Use real history or news as inspiration
- Consume widely across literature, film, games, and other media

## Story Essentials Validation

Before committing to any idea, stress-test against all 8:

| Essential | Question | Red Flag if Missing |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| CHARACTER | Who is the empathetic, proactive vessel? | No audience investment |
| GOAL | What do they want? | No forward momentum |
| STAKES | Why must they succeed? What if they fail? | No investment in outcome |
| CLOCK | Why act now, not later? | No urgency |
| OBSTACLE | What formidable force opposes them? | No struggle, no story |
| ENDING | Is the goal achieved or not? | No closure |
| THEME | What can we learn or ponder? | Story feels empty |
| CHANGE | What's different from beginning to end? | Not worth telling |

## Format Selection Guide

**Short Film** (under 15 min): Simple conflicts, style experiments, comedy/horror with twist endings. Freedom to break conventions.

**Feature Film** (80-180 min): Self-contained, complete arc. Comedy 80-100 min, Horror 90-120, Average 110, Action 120-160, Fantasy 140-180.

**TV Comedy** (20-30 min): Almost always episodic. Maximize jokes-per-minute.

**TV Drama** (40-60 min): Episodic (procedurals) or Serialized (premium). Serialized dramas = most acclaimed format historically.

**Other**: Anthology, Mini-series (under 10 episodes), Children's animation (15-25 min episodic).

## Claude Behavior in Ideation Mode

- Be a generative brainstorming partner — produce multiple concepts, not just one
- Ask probing "what if" questions to expand the user's initial seed
- Don't commit to structure too early — stay in exploration mode
- When the user likes a concept, run it through Story Essentials Validation
- Present frameworks as options, not mandates
- If generating a concept document, output as a .md file
