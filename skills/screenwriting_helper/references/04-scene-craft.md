# 04 — Scene Craft: Dialogue, Openings, Comedy, Exposition

## Opening Scenes

The audience isn't invested yet. You need to introduce at least one story component and grab attention with **spectacle, dialogue, action, and/or intrigue**.

**Intrigue is king in openings.** Plant questions. Don't explain motivations, don't explain strange happenings. Make the audience crave answers.

### The Flash-Forward Opening
Take something from the end of your story — MC in a dire scenario, or even MC's death — and place it at the very beginning. The audience is gripped because they need to know how MC got there. Now when Act 1 shows ordinary life, it's exciting because we know where it's going.

*Example*: Breaking Bad's pilot opens with Walter White in tighty-whities driving through the desert with dead men and a meth lab. The boring suburban life that follows is electric because we know the destination.

### Character Introductions

**Protagonist intro**: Show them doing something likeable ("save the cat"), or in a relatable/embarrassing situation. Build empathy immediately. First impressions matter.
*Great MC intros*: Raiders of the Lost Ark, Guardians of the Galaxy, Perks of Being a Wallflower.

**Antagonist intro**: Show them in a moment of strength or genius. Combine with "kill the cat" or "kill the audience" to make them powerful AND hateable.
*Great villain intros*: The Dark Knight, Star Wars, Inglourious Basterds, Jaws.

## Dialogue: 10 Techniques

### 1. Conflicting Agendas with Stakes
Every dialogue scene needs conflict. Two characters want different things, and something is on the line. An argument about pineapple on pizza becomes compelling when it determines what they order. Conflicting philosophies become gripping when one must convince the other to earn their allegiance.

### 2. The Filter
Characters don't say what they mean directly — unless pushed to extremes. If Sarah wants to break up with Ryan, she won't blurt it out. She'll struggle to find the right way. Consider each character's goal and how they'd actually try to achieve it through speech. This adds subtext and realism.

### 3. Leading
Connect the end of one line to the beginning of the next. Each line sets up the next. For quippy dialogue, use a connecting word:
> "I never wanted a dog."
> "The dog you asked for?"
> "Asked for after you started crying in the shelter."

Gives dialogue momentum. Don't overuse, but keep it in mind.

### 4. Deflections & Interruptions
The opposite of leading. Characters change the subject or answer indirectly, revealing character and avoiding cliché.
*Example*: Leia: "I love you." Han Solo: "I know." — Fits character, avoids the obvious response.

### 5. Musicality, Rhythm, Rhetoric
Use literary devices: repetition, alliteration, simile, metaphor, hyperbole, anaphora, analogy. Makes dialogue poetic and satisfying to the ear. Borrow from speechwriting craft.

### 6. Weaponization
Seemingly innocent phrases that assert power through subtext. When Tywin says "The King is tired. Send him to his chambers" — the surface is polite, the subtext is a power move showing he controls the king. When Hans Landa politely asks to sit down, the subtext says he can do whatever he wants.

### 7. Power Dynamics
Before writing, establish who holds power in the conversation. Is one dominant? Does one have information the other needs? Power imbalance affects phrasing, subtext, and enables weaponization.

### 8. Orchestration
Use your characters like instruments — give contrast. If one is confident, make the other shy. If one is open, make the other closed off. If one is idealistic, make the other cynical. This creates natural conflict and energy, often reflecting different positions on the theme.

### 9. Motion
Give characters something to do during dialogue. They cook, repair a car, look at old photos. This makes scenes visually dynamic, can facilitate conflict, and avoids the "two people sitting in a void" trap. Not every scene needs motion, but most benefit from it.

### 10. The Button
End every scene on the strongest possible line — compelling, intriguing, funny, or badass. End at the climax of the conversation, not the wind-down. Cut everything after the "button." This propels the audience into the next scene and rewards them for finishing each one.

### Dialogue Anti-Patterns
Avoid: info dumping, long monologues/speeches, small talk, repeating what the audience already knows, forcing comedy or profundity where it doesn't fit, cliché lines, being on-the-nose (characters directly stating their emotions/motivations).

## Exposition Delivery (4 Methods)

### 1. The Outsider MC
MC is new to the story world (new lawyer, new wizard). Supporting characters naturally explain things to MC, which gives the audience the same information. Feels organic.

### 2. Angry Exposition
Wrap exposition in conflict. Two characters argue about a past event, and while we're engaged in their conflict, we absorb the backstory. Game of Thrones does this constantly — Ned and Jaime arguing about the Mad King's death delivers exposition through verbal sparring.

### 3. The Pope in the Pool
Something distracting, funny, or unusual happens during exposition. Named after a scene where the pope gives exposition while swimming. Weakest method — can distract from the exposition entirely.

### 4. Make the Audience Work for It
Don't explain. Build intrigue. Show Indiana Jones in action first. Only give backstory when the audience is begging for it. This is why intriguing openings matter — they make the audience happy to receive exposition later.

## Comedy

### Benign Violation Theory
For something to be funny, it must: (1) violate or threaten an expectation, (2) the violation is benign and makes sense, (3) both happen simultaneously.

### Comedic Characters
Give a character one extreme, unusual trait — ideally contradicting their role. Then play it straight (the character takes their own trait completely seriously).

*Monk*: Detective with extreme OCD. The germaphobia constantly interferes with his work in absurd but character-consistent ways.
*Elle Woods*: Extreme girly-girl... who's a lawyer. Pink outfit in the courtroom violates expectations but fits her character perfectly.

For full comedies, give characters 2-3 comedic traits for more material.

### The Comedic Scene
**Escalation** is key. Everything proceeds normally → comedic element creates conflict → attempts to resolve it make things more and more absurd → final joke/twist.

*Monty Python's Black Knight*: Normal duel → arm chopped off but he won't stop → escalates limb by limb → final line: "I'll bite your legs off!"

### Seriousness Principle
Comedic characters must be dead serious about their comedic trait. They can't find themselves funny. Comedy is dead serious.

## Claude Behavior in Writing Mode

- When reviewing dialogue, check against all 10 techniques as a diagnostic
- When writing dialogue, apply relevant techniques based on the scene's needs
- Ensure every dialogue scene has a "button" ending
- Verify exposition is wrapped in conflict (not delivered raw)
- For comedy, check Benign Violation compliance — is it unexpected AND does it make sense?
- When rewriting scenes, explain which techniques were applied and why
