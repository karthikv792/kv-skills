# 05 — Tension Engine: Suspense, Information, Mystery, Setup/Payoff

## Tension, Suspense, and Surprise

### The Rubber Band Analogy
A dramatic question ("Will Lisa survive?") is like a stretched rubber band. The audience wants it released (good outcome). It could snap (bad outcome).

- **Tension** = the stretching. Obstacles and setbacks make the bad outcome look increasingly likely.
- **Suspense** = the holding. Prolonged uncertainty between outcomes.
- **Surprise** = the snap. Information the audience didn't have is suddenly revealed.

### Building Tension
Add obstacles, conflict, and setbacks. Show the audience you're not afraid to let things go horribly wrong. Kill beloved characters. Let secrets get out. Let smart plans crumble. The audience can never feel safe.

### Building Suspense
Raise a dramatic question and refuse to answer it. Complement with tension to make the wait agonizing.

*Bomb under the table*: MC doesn't know about the bomb. Audience does. MC keeps talking (tension rises). We wait to see if MC leaves in time (suspense).

### Delivering Surprise
Give knowledge the audience didn't have, answering a question they didn't know to ask. Make them confident they understand, then reveal something that recontextualizes everything — but feels earned because clues were there all along.

## The Four Info-States (Anagnorisis Framework)

| # | Audience | Character | Creates |
|---|----------|-----------|---------|
| 1 | Knows | Doesn't know | **Anticipation** |
| 2 | Doesn't know | Doesn't know | **Ignorance** (false continuity) |
| 3 | Doesn't know | Knows | **Secret** |
| 4 | Knows | Knows | **Continuity** |

### Three Emotional Transitions (Info-states 1-3 → 4)

**Anticipation → Continuity = RELIEF**
Audience knew about a threat the character didn't. When character encounters it, the waiting ends.

**Ignorance → Continuity = SURPRISE**
Neither knew. The reveal shatters false confidence but feels earned through foreshadowing.
*Example*: Game of Thrones' major deaths — everything seems fine until a hidden plan executes.

**Secret → Continuity = REVEAL**
Character knew something audience didn't. Reveal recontextualizes past actions.
*Example*: In The Whale, Charlie refuses to see a doctor. Audience assumes poverty. Reveal: he's saving every dollar for his estranged daughter.

Use deliberately: Thrillers live on anticipation → relief. Mysteries on ignorance → surprise. Character dramas on secret → reveal.

## Mystery & Intrigue

**Core mechanic**: Raise questions, don't immediately answer them. Unanswered things must have consequences — they affect plot, characters, or stakes.

**Building intrigue**: Refuse to explain important decisions/events. Plan outside-in (know everything, reveal slowly). Drip-feed answers so audience always has unanswered questions.

**The satisfying answer must be**: Unexpected, Fitting (makes sense with everything seen), Explanatory (recontextualizes past events), Consequential (changes trajectory).

*Example*: Snape's full backstory explains his killing of Dumbledore, his allegiance, his protection of Harry — all recontextualized by his love for Lily.

## Setup & Payoff

### Four Types
- **Practical**: Object in Act 1 becomes useful later (Chekhov's gun)
- **Emotional**: Line/moment repeated in different emotional context
- **Comedic**: Running gags, or serious line that becomes punchline
- **Dramatic**: Every action has consequences (MC cheats → it must come back)

### Multiple Setups
2+ setups before each payoff to ensure audience catches it and maximize buildup.

### Convergence of Payoffs
Save most payoffs for the ending. Multiple payoffs converging in quick succession creates completeness.

*Example*: Interstellar — ghost, watch, wormhole handshake. All set up in Act 1, all pay off in the finale.

---

## Story World as Conflict Generator

**Setting** = time/place + overarching conflict. **Story World** = specific slice of life the story focuses on. Story world shapes characters and generates conflict naturally. Stay focused within it and mine all its potential.

---

## Claude Behavior in Tension Mode

- Map major plot points to info-states
- Plan transitions for desired emotional response
- Track all open questions and planned answers
- Verify every setup has a payoff (and vice versa)
- Check mystery answers: unexpected + fitting + explanatory + consequential
- Verify convergence of payoffs at climax
